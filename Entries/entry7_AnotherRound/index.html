Year: 2020

Director: Thomas Vinterberg

“To dare is to lose one's footing momentarily. Not to dare is to lose oneself.”

Synopsis

Mikkelsen is Martin, a bored, indifferent high-school teacher who, like his closest colleagues, is trapped in the creeping throes of a midlife crisis – a feeling that the glass is now half empty. Inspired by Norwegian psychiatrist Finn Skårderud’s suggestion that the human body has an inbuilt alcohol deficiency, Martin and three close friends embark on a reckless experiment: to see if daytime drinking can help them become better versions of themselves – to learn to live again.

Insight on the film

Exploration of Midlife Crisis and Existential Questions: The characters in Another Round confront stagnation in their personal and professional lives. By experimenting with alcohol, they seek to reclaim lost excitement and purpose, which resonates with audiences facing similar crises or moments of self-reflection.

Balance of Humor and Tragedy: The film skillfully blends humor with more tragic elements, allowing audiences to experience a spectrum of emotions and to see the characters as multidimensional and relatable, regardless of their flaws. This balance makes it a unique viewing experience and emphasizes the unpredictability of life and human reactions to it.

Critical Take on Substance Dependence and Escapism: Although the characters' experiment is initially portrayed as liberating, it soon highlights the dangers of dependency and escapism. The film subtly invites viewers to reflect on their ways of coping with stress or dissatisfaction, encouraging a more mindful approach to handling life's challenges.

Celebration of Life's Complexity: The final scenes—particularly the jubilant dance sequence—underscore a bittersweet celebration of life, with all its joys, mistakes, and regrets. Audiences are left with a powerful message about the importance of embracing imperfection and living fully, even if the journey includes moments of loss or missteps.

Another Round encourages introspection and empathy, inviting audiences to consider the ways they seek meaning and connection in their lives. Its global popularity reflects how universal these themes are and how they resonate deeply across different cultures and age groups.

What I loved

The film started as being a tribute to alcohol and then quite quickly Vinterberg and Mikkelsen realized that they had to touch upon the other side of the coin as well. But not just make a film about the dark side of drinking because that's been done numerous times to perfection. They also did not want to be immoral about it. They wanted to remind people that human beings have been drinking for 7,000 years. There must be a reason - whether to take the load off your shoulders or to get more inspired or to somebody who doesn't have the balls to pick up the phone and make that phone call.

Vinterberg normally questioned – when there's a crowd of people asking him this question – "How many of you met your spouse without alcohol involved?" There's not a lot of hands coming up. We all know how beautiful it can be, and how richer a conversation can be after a couple of glasses. We also know after a bottle how it's not that interesting anymore. But we know if you can hit a perfect zone, as we argue in the film, there is something to gain there. I mean, look at Winston Churchill, and what he did while he was completely soaked. Look at Ernest Hemingway and what he did while drunk. So there is something there, and that's what the movie saying. It can go both ways. Is it bad? Or is it good? The answer is "Yes."

Brief on production

Director: Vinterberg treats the tale with a light hand, though he’s [revealed in interviews that personal tragedy] was an impetus for its story; his daughter Ida, who convinced him to make the film, was killed in a car accident four days into filming, and Vinterberg’s response was to rework the script to make it more life-affirming.

Acting: Vinterberg also makes excellent use of Mikkelsen’s own past as a dancer, mining the incongruously fluid body language of a tall, thickset man with a jaw that seems carved out of lead. There is a scene early in the film where your character, who is clearly reluctant to drink, is pressured into a sip of vodka at a dinner with friends. The look of resigned despair on your face was my favorite moment of the film. It was fascinating that you were able to convey so much drama while maintaining such an otherwise passive expression.

Cinematographer: Shortly after the experiment begins, Vinterberg stages a scene in Martin’s classroom, where he’s engaging with his students in a way he clearly hasn’t in years. He’s getting them involved with vibrant conversation and new ways to look at history. He’s smiling in that very Mads way. What’s brilliant about the scene is how Vinterberg and cinematographer [Sturla Brandth Grøvlen] replicate that slightly wobbly feeling that comes after just a strong drink or two. Martin is nowhere near blacking out or doing anything embarrassing, but the slightly unsteady camera swoops in for a close-up and then back out again in the inconsistent way that the world sometimes does after a couple glasses of wine—the filmmaking coming to life like how Martin is with his new buzz on life. It’s indicative of the high craft on display here as the visual language subtly matches the character’s journey.
